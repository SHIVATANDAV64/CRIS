## Core Mechanism
The paper provides a rigorous mathematical characterization of transformers with **hardmax self-attention** and normalization sublayers by treating them as **discrete-time dynamical systems** describing point evolution in Euclidean space. The key technical insight is a **geometric interpretation of self-attention based on hyperplane separation**: each attention head defines a separating hyperplane, and hardmax attention selects the single token with maximum projection onto this hyperplane. As the number of layers increases to infinity, the authors prove that transformer inputs **asymptotically converge to a clustered equilibrium** determined by special points called **leaders** — tokens that retain their identity through the transformation and serve as attractors for other tokens.

## Key Insight
The effectiveness of transformers for language tasks stems from their **convergence to clustered equilibria** in the infinite-layer limit. Leader tokens (words carrying semantic meaning) act as **attractors**, pulling other tokens (meaningless words, stopwords) toward them in the embedding space. This creates interpretable clusters where context is captured by which leader a token clusters around, rather than complex learned parameters. The mathematical framework makes this entirely transparent: the model's behavior is fully determined by the geometry of the attention hyperplanes.

## Domain-Blind Abstraction
A nested transformation system (repeatedly applying the same function across layers) eventually settles into stable configurations where points cluster around special "anchor" points. The anchors are selected by a competitive selection mechanism (hardmax) that chooses the most aligned point at each step. This creates a finite set of stable attractors that determine the fate of all other points in the system.
