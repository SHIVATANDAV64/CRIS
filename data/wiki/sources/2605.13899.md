## Core Mechanism
The paper introduces **frequency-space mechanics**, a representational framework that encodes proteins as **Mechanical Harmonics Graphs (MHGs)**. The key innovation is transforming a protein's vibrational dynamics into a graph structure without relying on sequence or atomic coordinates.

**How it works:**
1. Perform molecular dynamics simulations to obtain vibrational modes (eigenmodes) of the protein
2. Construct nodes from these vibrational modes
3. Create edges weighted by "octave alignment" — how well frequencies of different modes align harmonically (similar to musical octaves)
4. Project out the original atomic coordinates, creating a coordinate-free, scale-invariant latent mechanical space
5. Feed these static MHGs into a graph neural network (GNN) to predict Gene Ontology (GO) molecular function terms
6. Apply **Kuramoto entrainment** — a dynamical process that synchronizes oscillators based on their coupling — to the harmonic coupling graph to capture collective conformational dynamics

## Key Insight
**Vibrational physics alone encodes sufficient information for broad functional classification.** The fundamental principle is that proteins function through collective vibrational dynamics, and these dynamics create characteristic "fingerprints" in frequency space that correlate with molecular function. By representing proteins purely in terms of their vibrational mode relationships (ignoring sequence and static structure), the paper demonstrates that functional information is preserved — a surprising result given that sequence homology is the traditional gold standard for function inference.

## Domain-Blind Abstraction
Represent a system as a graph where nodes are oscillatory modes and edges represent harmonic coupling strength based on frequency relationships. Train a neural network on these graphs to predict system properties. Optionally, apply dynamical synchronization (entrainment) to capture state-dependent behavior.
