## Core Mechanism

The key innovation is introducing a **geometric representation dimension** parameter m that characterizes the discrete learning problem. By adjusting m based on sample size N, the bounds adapt to the problem's geometric structure:
- For small m: recovers the standard O(1/N^{1/2}) dimension-free worst-case rate
- For larger m optimized for given N: yields significantly tighter bounds for practical sample sizes

The theoretical foundation relies on **non-asymptotic concentration of measure** results for finite metric spaces, established using **metric embedding arguments** that map the discrete problem structure into favorable geometric configurations.

## Key Insight
The constant c in classical generalization bounds grows with ambient dimension d and machine precision, making bounds vacuous for realistic sample sizes. The insight is that the **geometric representation dimension m** (distinct from ambient dimension) provides a finer-grained measure of problem complexity that adapts to both the discretization structure and available data. Since c_m ∈ O(m^{1/2}) for discretized Euclidean domains, choosing m appropriately relative to N exploits the actual representational structure rather than worst-case assumptions.

By characterizing discretization complexity via a representation dimension parameter, generalization guarantees can be tightened by adapting to the actual effective complexity rather than assuming worst-case behavior. This is achieved through concentration of measure phenomena: as sample size grows, the probability mass concentrates around expected behavior, with the concentration rate depending on the geometric structure of the finite space.
