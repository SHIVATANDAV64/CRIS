## Core Mechanism
The paper proposes that AI systems achieve contestability through **computational argumentation** — a framework in which machines dynamically construct, exchange, and evaluate structured arguments. Specifically, the mechanism requires two interacting capabilities: (1) **progressive explanation**: machines iteratively justify their outputs and reasoning to human or machine interlocutors, unpacking decisions step-by-step rather than producing opaque verdicts; and (2) **argument-responsive revision**: machines assess counterarguments (grounds for contestation) raised by humans or other machines, and formally revise their decision-making processes when challenges are found to be justified. The system operates as a dialectical loop — an agent presents a decision with supporting arguments, receives objections, evaluates them according to argumentation semantics (e.g., defeat relations, argument strength), and updates or defends its conclusion accordingly.

## Key Insight
Current AI systems are predominantly **static**: once trained or deployed, they produce outputs without any mechanism for structured challenge and response. The key insight is that contestability is not merely about transparency (showing how a decision was made) but about enabling a **structured adversarial exchange** where reasons can be weighed against counter-reasons. Computational argumentation provides exactly this — a formal, well-studied framework for representing competing claims, their relationships (support, defeat), and principled methods for determining which arguments prevail. This makes it a natural computational backbone for the explain-exchange-revise cycle that contestability demands.

## Domain-Blind Abstraction
At the most abstract level, this work addresses the problem of building **systems that can justify their conclusions, receive structured challenges to those conclusions, and revise their reasoning in light of those challenges**. The core computational pattern is a dialectical cycle: an agent commits to a position supported by reasons → a challenger presents counter-reasons → the system evaluates the relative strength of competing reason sets → the system either maintains, modifies, or retracts its original position. This pattern is domain-independent — it describes any system that must reason under disagreement and update through structured discourse rather than only through new data ingestion.

## Cross-Domain Potential
- [[Legal AI]]: Legal reasoning is inherently argumentative — attorneys present competing interpretations of law and evidence. Computational argumentation maps naturally onto adversarial legal processes, enabling AI tools that can argue cases and respond to judicial challenges.
- [[Medical Decision Support]]: Clinical decisions involve weighing competing diagnoses and treatment plans. An argumentation-based system could present its diagnostic reasoning to physicians, accept counter-evidence (e.g., contradictory lab results), and formally revise its conclusions.
- [[Policy Analysis and Governance]]: Public policy decisions involve balancing competing stakeholder interests and values. Argumentation frameworks can model policy proposals as arguments, stakeholder objections as counter-arguments, and support transparent, contestable policy recommendation.
- [[Scientific Peer Review]]: The peer review process is a structured form of argumentation where reviewers contest claims and authors respond. Computational argumentation could formalize this exchange, enabling AI systems to participate in or facilitate scholarly debate.
